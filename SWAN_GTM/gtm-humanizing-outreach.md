# Humanizing Outreach

This skill does two jobs at once: it strips the patterns that signal a message was
generated by a model, and it adds the personality and specificity that signal a
real person chose to send it. Avoiding AI tells is only half the work — sterile,
voiceless copy is just as obvious as slop. Every message needs a human behind it.

Apply this **while drafting**, not only as a cleanup pass. The patterns are easier
to avoid than to remove.

## The self-audit loop

After drafting any message, run this before you finalize it:

**Step 1 — Ask: "What makes this obviously AI-generated?"** Answer honestly. List
the tells that are still there: rhythm too uniform, word choices too polished,
opener too generic, no real point of view.

**Step 2 — Ask: "Now make it not obviously AI-generated."** Revise based on that
audit. Usually this means cutting the opener, making the middle more specific, and
shortening the close.

This loop catches what mechanical pattern-checking misses — the overall *feel* of
the message. The pattern catalog tells you what to look for; the loop tells you
whether you actually fixed it.

## Personality and soul

A message can be technically clean and still read as AI. The fix is a human behind
it. Signs of soulless writing:

- Every sentence is the same length and structure.
- No reaction to the specific thing being referenced — just a neutral description of it.
- No point of view, just reporting.
- The close could be pasted onto any message to any prospect.
- It reads like a press release.

How to add voice:

- **Have an opinion.** Don't just describe what you noticed — react to it. "I keep
  coming back to this" beats neutrally listing facts.
- **Vary rhythm.** Short punchy sentences. Then longer ones that take their time
  getting where they're going. Uniform sentence length is the clearest AI tell.
- **Be specific about feelings.** Not "this is interesting" but "there's something
  telling about a company that just added three SDRs without fixing conversion."
- **Use "I" when it fits.** First person is honest, not unprofessional.
- **Acknowledge complexity.** "This is impressive but also kind of a weird move"
  beats a neutral summary.
- **One idea per message.** Outreach that covers three angles covers zero.

Before (clean but soulless):
> We help sales teams improve efficiency. Our platform has been used by hundreds of
> companies. The results speak for themselves.

After (has a pulse):
> Most teams we talk to aren't slow because of effort — they're slow because the
> reps are triaging inbound manually. That's the thing we fix.

## Banned vocabulary

Never use these. Not even once. When several co-occur in one message, it reads like
raw prompt output.

- **Significance inflation:** pivotal, transformative, testament, groundbreaking,
  enduring, evolving landscape, foster, underscores, marks a shift, setting the
  stage for, indelible mark, vital role
- **AI filler:** additionally, crucial, delve, enhance, highlight (verb), intricate,
  key (adjective), showcase, tapestry, vibrant, valuable, align with, garner,
  interplay
- **Promotional:** nestled, breathtaking, renowned, boasts, stunning, cutting-edge,
  rich (figurative), profound
- **Copula avoidance:** "serves as", "stands as", "functions as", "marks a" — use
  "is" / "are" / "has" instead.

## High-frequency rules to internalize

- **No semicolons. Full ban.** Real people don't use them in emails or DMs.
- **Max one em dash per message.** Models overuse them badly.
- **The 20-word rule.** Any sentence over 20 words should almost always be split in two.
- **Cut "just."** "Just wanted to follow up" reads apologetic, not considerate.
- **Kill throat-clearing openers.** "One thing we've seen across companies like
  yours is..." — just make the observation.
- **No emojis** in cold or warm outreach.

## The soul check

Before finalizing any message, ask: does this sound like a real person chose to send
it, or like a template that got filled in? It lacks soul if:

- Every sentence is the same structure and length.
- There's no reaction to the specific signal — just a description of it.
- The close could be pasted onto any message to any prospect.
- Reading it aloud sounds like a press release.

If any of these are true, rewrite the worst offender. One human moment — a specific
observation, a direct opinion, an honest ask — is enough to change the read on the
whole message.

# Reference — The 30-Pattern Catalog

Thirty named anti-patterns that make outreach sound generated. Scan for the ones
most likely to appear in the message you're drafting.

## 1. Significance inflation
Puffing up importance — "pivotal moment," "reflects broader trends." Sounds like a Wikipedia lede.

## 2. Promotional / advertisement-like language
Tourist-brochure tone — "nestled at the intersection of...", "groundbreaking platform..."

## 3. Vague attributions and weasel words
"Industry experts have noted..." Kills credibility instantly.

## 4. Superficial -ing phrases
Tacking participles to fake depth — "highlighting," "underscoring," "showcasing."

## 5. Overused AI vocabulary
Hit list: actually, additionally, align with, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring,
enhance, fostering, garner, highlight (verb), interplay, intricate, key (adjective),
landscape (abstract), pivotal, showcase, tapestry, testament, underscore, valuable, vibrant.

## 6. Copula avoidance
Replace "serves as," "stands as," "functions as," "boasts" with "is" / "are" / "has".

## 7. Negative parallelisms
"It's not just about X, it's about Y." Template marker.

## 8. Rule of three overuse
"Find, engage, and convert." Reads formulaic.

## 9. Elegant variation (synonym cycling)
Don't call the same thing three different names — platform / tool / solution.

## 10. Passive voice and subjectless fragments
"Results are delivered automatically. No configuration required." → "The system delivers results. You don't need to configure anything."

## 11. Em dash overuse
Max one per message.

## 12. Boldface overuse
Bolding mechanically in a short email looks like a template.

## 13. Inline-header lists
Bullets where each item starts **Label:** ... — reads like a product spec sheet.

## 14. Signposting and announcements
"I wanted to reach out because..." Announces instead of saying. Cut it.

## 15. Sycophantic and servile tone
"Hope this finds you well!" Cut. Start with the signal.

## 16. Filler phrases
- "In order to" → "To"
- "Due to the fact that" → "Because"
- "At this point in time" → "Now"
- "Has the ability to" → "Can"
- "It is important to note that" → cut it

## 17. Excessive hedging
"It could potentially possibly be argued..." → "This usually moves pipeline 20-30% in 90 days."

## 18. Generic positive conclusions
"This is an exciting opportunity for growth." Empty sentiment.

## 19. Persuasive authority tropes
"As industry leaders know..." Borrowed authority.

## 20. Emojis in outreach
None in cold or warm outreach.

## 21. Hyphenated word-pair overuse
"forward-thinking, data-driven, results-oriented" stacked = generated.

## 22. Colon-separated insight statements
"The reality is this: companies need X." Setup adds nothing.

## 23. "Caught my attention" + participial restatement
Cut the meta-narrative.

## 24. First-person opener frequency (email only)
Don't start cold emails with "I."

## 25. "Just" as a weakener
"Just wanted to follow up." Cut "just."

## 26. Semicolons
Full ban in outreach.

## 27. Pedantic educator tone
"It's important to understand that..." You're not lecturing.

## 28. Feature lists dressed as observations
"I noticed your company is in SaaS, scaling fast, and likely facing data challenges..."
That's a feature list pretending to be research.

## 29. Vague spatial/transitional filler
"In today's landscape, going forward, at the end of the day..." Cut all of them.

## 30. Two-sentence test (20-word rule)
Over 20 words? Split it. No relative clauses bolted onto an already-complete thought.
