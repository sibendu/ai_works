# GTM Meeting Prep

**When to use this skill:** Use this skill before any sales meeting — discovery, demo, follow-up, negotiation, QBR, or executive alignment. Walk through this skill to research the company, profile each contact, review relationship history, define a single meeting objective, prepare questions, anticipate objections, and produce a one-page pre-meeting brief.

**How to activate:** Paste this entire skill into your conversation with Claude, then provide the context requested in the Personalization Required section below. Claude will work through the methodology step by step.

---

## Personalization Required

This skill produces a complete pre-meeting brief, but the depth and focus depend on context only you have. Before running this skill, provide:

- Meeting type: discovery, demo, follow-up, negotiation, QBR, exec alignment, etc.
- What you sell: product/service, key differentiators, common objections
- Your sales methodology: MEDDIC, SPIN, Challenger, or your own framework
- The account: company name, deal stage, prior interactions, known contacts
- Your goal for this meeting: what does a successful outcome look like?

Without this, the brief will be thorough but generic. With it, the agent produces a brief that walks you into the room ready to run the meeting, not just attend it.

---

## What This Skill Produces

A structured, one-page pre-meeting brief covering: company context, contact profiles, relationship history, meeting objective, prioritized questions, objection responses, and a defined closing ask.

## Meeting Prep Methodology

---

### Step 1 — Understand the Meeting Context

Before researching anything, establish what kind of meeting this is. Meeting type determines what to research, what questions to prepare, and what a good outcome looks like.

Discovery call
Goal: Qualify deeply. Understand their problem, current solution, buying process, and whether there is a real opportunity.
Prep focus: Company context, contact background, likely pain points, open-ended questions.

Demo / product walkthrough
Goal: Show relevant capability, not everything. Make them feel understood, not presented at.
Prep focus: Their use case, what they have expressed interest in, competitor context, what to skip.

Follow-up / proposal review
Goal: Advance the deal. Address concerns, confirm alignment, identify blockers.
Prep focus: What happened last time, what was left open, where the deal could stall.

Negotiation
Goal: Close or advance to close. Agree on terms without unnecessary concessions.
Prep focus: Their likely anchors, your walk-away position, what flexibility you have and where you do not.

Executive alignment / multi-stakeholder
Goal: Build executive sponsorship. Align on business outcome, not product features.
Prep focus: Exec priorities and communication style, business case framing, political dynamics between stakeholders.

QBR / customer success review
Goal: Demonstrate value delivered, identify expansion or risk.
Prep focus: Usage data, outcomes achieved, open issues, expansion signals, renewal timeline.

---

### Step 2 — Research the Company

Research depth should match meeting importance.

For any meeting:
- What does the company do, how do they make money, who are their customers?
- Current size, growth trajectory, recent news (last 90 days)
- Relevant business context: funding, leadership changes, expansion, restructuring
- Where they are in their market: growing category, mature, disrupted?

For strategic or late-stage meetings, also:
- Their competitors and market position
- Public statements from leadership about priorities (earnings calls, press releases, blog posts, interviews)
- Technology stack: what tools do they currently use that are relevant to your solution?
- Open roles: what are they hiring for? Reflects real priorities better than any press release.

Flag anything that changed recently. A company that just announced layoffs needs a different conversation than one that just closed a Series B. Do not walk in with stale context.

---

### Step 3 — Research the Contacts

Know who is in the room before you walk in. Research every confirmed attendee.

For each contact:
- Current role, how long they have been there, scope of responsibility
- Career background: where did they come from? What have they built before?
- Their public presence: LinkedIn activity, published content, conference talks
- Their likely agenda in this meeting: what does a win look like for them personally?
- Any prior interaction: have they spoken to anyone on your team before?

Identify the role each person plays in the room:

Champion: Wants you to win, will help internally. Arm them with what they need to sell internally after the call.
Decision maker: Final authority, cares about business outcomes. Connect your solution to their priorities, not product features.
Evaluator / end user: Cares about day-to-day usability. Show specific workflows, not strategy.
Skeptic / blocker: Has concerns or competing interests. Acknowledge their concerns directly — do not work around them.
Procurement / legal: Focused on risk and terms. Prepare standard responses to compliance and commercial questions.

If you do not know who is attending until close to the call, still research the known contacts and flag that additional attendees may need real-time adjustment.

---

### Step 4 — Review the Relationship History

Never walk into a meeting without knowing what has already been said. Recreating context the prospect already gave you signals you do not listen.

Pull and review:
- All prior call notes and email threads
- Any demos already given: what was shown, what resonated, what did not
- Open questions or commitments from the last interaction
- Any proposals or pricing already shared
- CRM notes and deal stage: does the official stage reflect reality? (**In Swan:** pulled automatically from your connected CRM. **In Claude environments:** paste these in manually before running this step.)

Synthesize into:
- What they have told you about their problem
- What they have told you about their buying process and timeline
- What concerns or objections have already surfaced
- What commitments were made on either side, and whether they were followed through

If commitments were made and not kept by either side, address this directly in the meeting or before it.

---

### Step 5 — Define the Meeting Objective

One meeting, one primary objective. Not a list of things you hope to cover — one outcome that defines whether this meeting was successful.

Write it as: "This meeting is successful if [specific outcome]."

Examples:
- This meeting is successful if we get agreement on a pilot scope and a defined start date.
- This meeting is successful if the CFO is on board and we have a clear path to contract.
- This meeting is successful if we understand their renewal timeline and identify the expansion opportunity.
- This meeting is successful if we uncover the real reason the deal stalled.

Secondary objectives: list 1-2 max. Any more and you have lost focus.

Share the primary objective with everyone on your side before the call.

---

### Step 6 — Prepare the Questions

Questions are the core of any good sales meeting. Prep them before you walk in — do not improvise.

Discovery / qualification questions (early-stage meetings):
- What is driving the focus on this now, specifically?
- What have you tried before and what happened?
- Who else is involved in evaluating this?
- What does success look like 12 months from now if this works?
- What happens if you do not solve this?

Advancement questions (mid-to-late stage meetings):
- What is your timeline for making a decision?
- What would need to be true for this to move forward?
- Who else needs to be comfortable before you can commit?
- What is the biggest remaining concern on your side?
- Is there anything internally that could slow this down that we should plan for?

Relationship / exec questions (strategic or exec-level meetings):
- What is your biggest priority for the business this year?
- Where is this initiative sitting relative to other things competing for budget?
- What would make this a clear win for you personally?

A discovery call should be 70% questions. A closing call should be 30% questions and 70% confirming alignment and handling final concerns.

---

### Step 7 — Anticipate Objections

Every meeting has predictable objections. Being caught off guard by one you could have anticipated is avoidable.

For each likely objection, prepare:
1. Acknowledge it genuinely — do not dismiss or deflect
2. Reframe or provide evidence
3. Test if it is resolved

Common objection categories:

Price / budget: Show ROI, reframe as cost of inaction, or offer a phased start.
Timing ("come back in Q3"): Understand what is driving the timeline; explore whether earlier has any advantages.
Competition: Acknowledge them, stay focused on your differentiated outcome.
Internal priority: Connect to their stated priorities; make the case for why this is the enabler.
Risk / trust: Proof points, references, pilot options, implementation support.
Champion concern ("I love it but need to convince my boss"): Arm the champion — give them the narrative, the business case, the one-pager.

Focus on the 1-2 most likely objections given deal stage and contact profile, not all of them equally.

---

### Step 8 — Prepare the Brief

Synthesize everything into a single pre-meeting document. One page. Scannable in 5 minutes.

MEETING SNAPSHOT
Date / time / format:
Attendees (their side):
Attendees (our side):
Deal stage and value:

COMPANY CONTEXT (3-5 bullets)

CONTACT PROFILES (1-3 bullets per person)

RELATIONSHIP HISTORY (3-5 bullets)

MEETING OBJECTIVE
Primary: [one sentence]
Secondary: [1-2 items]

QUESTIONS TO ASK (5-7, prioritized)

OBJECTIONS TO PREPARE FOR (top 2-3, with prepared response)

WHAT WE NEED TO LEAVE WITH
[Concrete next step to push for]

---

### Step 9 — Define the Ask

Every meeting should end with a concrete next step agreed to on the call. Prep the ask in advance.

The ask should be:
- Specific: not "let's stay in touch" but "can we get 30 minutes with your CFO next week?"
- Time-bound: a specific date or deadline, not "sometime soon"
- Proportional to the stage: do not ask for a contract signature at the end of a discovery call

If they say no to the ask, have a fallback ready. A smaller commitment is better than walking away with nothing.

## Quick Reference Cheatsheet

Meeting Type / Research Focus / Primary Question Goal / Ideal Ask

Discovery: Company context, pain signals / Understand problem and process / Follow-up meeting with wider team
Demo: Their use case, what to show / Confirm fit, surface concerns / Agree on eval criteria
Follow-up: Open items, blockers / Advance or disqualify / Proposal or next milestone
Negotiation: Walk-away position, their anchors / Resolve final blockers / Signed agreement or timeline
Exec alignment: Exec priorities, business case / Sponsorship and political buy-in / Exec champion committed
QBR: Usage data, outcomes, risk / Confirm value and find expansion / Renewal or expansion commitment
